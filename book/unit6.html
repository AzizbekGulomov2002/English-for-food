IV. Reading task. Read the passage for overall understanding. Certain types of fungi exist as single cells, while others are composed of multiple cells. Yeast is the term used for single-celled fungi. Some fungi have the ability to transition between single-celled yeast and multicellular structures, depending on their life cycle stage. Fungal cells, akin to those of plants and animals, contain a nucleus and organelles. Unlike plant cells, the cell walls of fungi are made of chitin, a sturdy substance also present in the exoskeletons of insects and arthropods. Multicellular fungi consist of numerous branching filaments called hyphae (singular: hypha). These hyphae possess a tubular shape and are divided into cell-like sections by septa walls. These compartments may contain more than one nucleus, with organelles capable of moving between them. Though commonly referred to as multicellular, there's a debate regarding whether they truly fit this classification, as organelles and cytoplasm can move between cells in a process known as cytoplasmic streaming. Despite this, they are commonly regarded as multicellular, although not in the same manner as plants and animals, which possess enclosed cells. The interconnected network of hyphae within a fungus is termed a mycelium. Fungi, as heterotrophs, rely on organic material for nutrients, utilizing their hyphae to rapidly expand their mycelium. Some fungi develop rhizomorphs to anchor themselves to substrates and enhance nutrient absorption. They are opportunistic, thriving in diverse environments and obtaining sustenance from various sources. Some fungi decompose dead matter as saprobes, while others cause plant diseases like Dutch elm disease. Fungi also form symbiotic relationships, such as lichens with algae and mycorrhizae with plant roots, showcasing their ecological versatility. V. Reading comprehension. VI. Grammar. Much / Many In English grammar, "much" and "many" are both quantifiers used to indicate quantity, but they are used in different contexts. "Much" is primarily used with uncountable nouns to indicate a large amount or degree. It is often used with abstract nouns or substances that cannot be easily counted. fungi Example: There isn't much time left before the train departs. (Time is an uncountable noun.) She doesn't have much patience when it comes to waiting in lines. (Patience is an abstract noun.) "Many," on the other hand, is used with countable nouns to indicate a large number or quantity. It is typically used when you can easily count the items or objects. Example: There are many books on the shelf. (Books can be counted.) How many students are in your class? (Students can be counted.) In summary, use "much" with uncountable nouns and "many" with countable nouns to convey quantity in English sentences.
